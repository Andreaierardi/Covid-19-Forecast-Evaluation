Testing the predictive accuracy of COVID-19 forecasts

We test the predictive accuracy of forecasts for the cumulative number of COVID-19 fatalities produced by several modelling teams and collected by the United States Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC)

The tests rely on fixed-smoothing asymptotics that allow for accurate testing even in small samples, see Coroneo and Iacone (2020).

For more details see the working paper "Testing the predictive accuracy of COVID-19 forecasts" , by Laura Coroneo, Fabrizio Iacone, Alessia Paccagnini and Paulo Santos Monteiro.

Results

Forecast evaluation for the number of cumulative deaths in the US made by eight forecasting teams:

  • Columbia University (Co)
  • Johns Hopkins University, Infectious Disease Dynamics Lab (JH)
  • Los Alamos National Laboratory (LA)
  • Massachusetts Institute of Technology, Operations Research Center (MI)
  • Northeastern University, Laboratory for the Modeling of Biological and Socio-technical Systems (MO)
  • University of California, Los Angeles (UC)
  • University of Massachusetts, Amherst (UM)
  • Youyang Gu (COVID-Projections) (YY)
We also report results for the Ensemble (En) forecast published by the CDC.

Results for the United States for predictions made from May 18, 2020 to August 24, 2020 are reported below.

The figure reports the test statistic for the test of equal predictive accuracy using the weighted covariance estimator (WCE) of the long run variance and fixed-b asymptotics. The benchmark is a second degree polynomial model fitted on a rolling window of 5 observations. A positive value of the test statistic indicates lower loss for the forecaster, i.e. better performance of the forecaster relative to the polynomial model. Different loss functions are reported with different markers: plus refers to a quadratic loss function, diamond to the absolute loss function, filled circle to the absolute percentage loss function and empty circle to the asymmetric loss function. The dotted, dashed and continuous red horizontal lines denote respectively the 20%, 10% and 5% significance levels. The forecast horizons h are 1, 2, 3 and 4 weeks ahead.
